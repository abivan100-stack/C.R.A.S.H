Why CRASH exists

You can't fix the crashes
you can't see.

Road crashes are among the leading causes of unnatural death in India — and nearly every one happens at a place someone could have flagged in advance. The records already exist. What's missing is a way to read them.

The concentration

Risk isn't spread evenly

A small set of junctions carries a large share of the serious crashes. Fix the worst ten first and you cut the most harm for the least spend — instead of thinning budgets across the whole city.

The blind spot

Counts hide severity

Ten minor scrapes are not one fatal collision. Ranking by raw volume points enforcement at the busy corners, not the deadly ones. Severity-weighting surfaces where people are actually being killed.

The signal

Patterns name the fix

A night-time, rain-linked, or high-speed signature tells you which intervention will work — lighting, drainage, or traffic calming. Without the pattern, spending is a guess.

The case

Evidence wins budgets

"This corner feels dangerous" doesn't get funded. A ranked, transparent, severity-weighted map turns a hunch into a decision a council or police chief can sign off — and defend.

How the risk score works

From raw incidents
to a ranked danger list

A transparent, four-step pipeline you can explain in a council meeting — no black-box scoring.

STEP 01

Grid the city

The bounding box is divided into ~250 m cells, so a "hotspot" is a real, walkable junction — not a vague neighbourhood.

cell ≈ 0.0022°
STEP 02

Weight by severity

Each cell scores the harm it causes, not just the count — a fatal crash weighs three times a slight one.

fatal ×3 · serious ×2 · slight ×1
STEP 03

Suppress & rank

Neighbouring cells are merged so the top 10 are ten distinct junctions, then ranked by score.

non-max suppression · top 10
STEP 04

Brief the fix

Time, weather, and severity patterns trigger plain-language intervention recommendations for each zone.

night>60% → lighting
